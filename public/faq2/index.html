Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Does a total station take pictures of people?

A: Modern total stations may have imaging features, but they are not cameras for personal use. Their purpose is precise measurement. Q: What does the surveying process involve behind the scenes?

A: Much of surveying occurs in the office. Surveyors begin with research and calculations to establish the framework for field measurements. After the field crew collects data, it returns to the office for analysis, verification, and final processing. Fieldwork is not always straightforward; surveyors navigate challenges such as missing or disturbed monuments, limited site access, environmental conditions, and other obstacles. This combination of office analysis and field expertise ensures accurate and defensible results.

Q: Why might a surveyor work far from my property?

A: Surveyors often need to evaluate surrounding properties to ensure accurate boundaries. This protects your property rights and those of neighboring properties, especially in older subdivisions where records or monuments may be missing or outdated.

Q: How does a surveyor handle older neighborhoods?

A: Many urban lots, especially in older areas like Santa Cruz, lack clear boundary markers. Surveyors verify measurements across multiple properties to confirm legal boundaries. When discrepancies are found, they are carefully resolved through research and precise field measurements.

Q: What is due diligence in surveying?

A: Due diligence includes reviewing historical records, checking existing boundary markers, and verifying measurements with neighboring properties. This ensures your survey is accurate, legally defensible, and aligned with surrounding lots.

Q: Why is accuracy so important?

A: Accurate surveys prevent future disputes, support property transactions, and ensure compliance with local regulations. By using precise instruments and professional analysis, surveyors establish reliable property boundaries that stand the test of time.

Q: What challenges do surveyors face in the field?

A: Fieldwork may be affected by destroyed or missing monuments, inaccessible areas, weather, and other unexpected obstacles. Professional surveyors adapt quickly to ensure measurements are still accurate, maintaining the integrity of the survey process.
